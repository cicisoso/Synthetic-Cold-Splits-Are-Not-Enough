\section{Conclusion}

We presented a benchmark-first study of distribution shift in DTI prediction. A 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} showed that cold-start regimes are already heterogeneous even under an external literature-based comparison panel: \texttt{HyperPCM} is strongest on the original synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, \texttt{blind\_start} has no reliable winner, and \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target} by mean \AUPRC{}. Review-driven robustness checks then made the split semantics sharper: a scaffold-drug probe removed the original \texttt{HyperPCM} edge, and a non-patent temporal probe preserved the \texttt{DTI-LM} advantage beyond the patent benchmark itself. A promoted 3-seed temporal/provenance benchmark on \texttt{BindingDB\_patent} still favored a different model family, with \texttt{DTI-LM} becoming the strongest model by mean \AUPRC{}, albeit by a small absolute margin. Pooled patent year-band and overlap-bucket analyses further showed that real OOD is structured rather than monolithic. Overall, these results indicate that, within the studied three-model panel and tested benchmarks, synthetic cold splits and real distribution shifts should not be treated as interchangeable evaluation settings. This manuscript should be read as a benchmark/resource case study rather than an exhaustive field-wide census. Future DTI papers should therefore report both synthetic and real-OOD evidence when making generalization claims.
