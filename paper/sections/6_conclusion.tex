\section{Conclusion}

We presented a benchmark-first study of distribution shift in DTI prediction. In the expanded five-model panel, a 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} showed that cold-start regimes are already heterogeneous: \texttt{RF} is strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start}, while \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target} by mean \AUPRC{}. Review-driven robustness checks then made the split semantics sharper: a scaffold-drug probe removed the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge, and a non-patent temporal probe showed that the expanded temporal ordering is not confined to the patent benchmark alone. Under matched 3-seed evaluation on both \texttt{BindingDB\_patent} and \texttt{BindingDB\_nonpatent\_Kd}, the strongest model is \texttt{RF}, while the \texttt{GraphDTA-style} graph-family probe remains competitive but not dominant. Pooled patent year-band and overlap-bucket analyses further showed that real OOD is structured rather than monolithic. Overall, these results indicate that, within the studied expanded panel and tested benchmarks, synthetic cold splits and real distribution shifts should not be treated as interchangeable evaluation settings, and that learner choice can matter as much as representation family. This manuscript should be read as a benchmark/resource case study rather than an exhaustive field-wide census. Future DTI papers should therefore report both synthetic and real-OOD evidence when making generalization claims.
