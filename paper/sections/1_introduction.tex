\section{Introduction}

Predicting drug-target interactions for unseen molecules or proteins is a central goal in computational drug discovery. Because truly prospective evaluation is difficult and expensive, many recent studies report held-out drug, target, or blind splits on datasets derived from BindingDB or DAVIS as proxies for inductive generalization \citep{liu2007bindingdb,davis2011kinase,ahmed2024dtilm,luo2024druglamp,ouyang2024pmmr,liu2025spdti,yu2025gsdti,zhao2025coldstartcpi}. These protocols are useful, but they also encourage a strong implicit assumption: if a method performs well on one cold-start split, then it is broadly robust to the kinds of shift encountered in deployment.

That assumption is weaker than it appears. Different split definitions remove different forms of overlap, and they may favor different model families. A pooled-LM baseline that looks strong under one synthetic exclusion rule may no longer be the most reliable option once the benchmark shifts in time, provenance, or assay composition. If these ranking changes occur even within a compact studied panel, benchmark design is not a technical footnote. It becomes part of the scientific claim.

This paper studies that question directly. Rather than proposing another universal architecture, we ask whether the ranking of an expanded DTI model panel is preserved across synthetic cold-start regimes and real distribution shifts. The goal is to test ranking stability under benchmark shift in a controlled case study, not to declare a universal field-wide leaderboard. Our panel begins with three representative systems: a fixed-feature MLP reference model (\texttt{base}) and two recent external literature baselines, \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}. We then add two deliberately different controls: a \texttt{RandomForest} classifier trained on the same Morgan+k-mer representation as \texttt{base}, and a \texttt{GraphDTA-style} graph-family baseline. Even on the synthetic reference panel, rankings are already heterogeneous: \texttt{RF} is strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start}, while \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target}.

We then contrast this synthetic picture with real temporal/provenance benchmarks built from BindingDB patent and non-patent time-based splits. The result is another ranking change. The synthetic panel does not point to a single dominant system, but the promoted temporal benchmarks strongly favor \texttt{RF}. Paired bootstrap confidence intervals support these temporal comparisons: \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM} remain positive on both \texttt{patent\_temporal} and \texttt{nonpatent\_temporal}. An earlier-cutoff variant of the patent benchmark (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_v2017}) had already preserved the same temporal ordering within the smaller panel, so the expanded results strengthen rather than overturn the original benchmark diagnosis.

To move beyond leaderboard-style reporting, we also diagnose the promoted temporal benchmark by year and overlap bucket. This analysis shows that temporal OOD is not simply ``harder cold-start.'' Instead, the gap is concentrated in specific year bands and overlap regimes, which helps explain why a method that looks strong under one split can lose under another. We further include matched 3-seed support on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} to test whether the same external panel remains stable across shifted target-cold settings. Recent molecular benchmark studies have made analogous arguments about OOD sensitivity in adjacent tasks \citep{ektefaie2024generalizability,shen2025ddiben,delafuente2025inductive}; our contribution is to show the same issue concretely in DTI and to release the benchmark suite as a reusable split-plus-evaluator resource for reliable DTI evaluation.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We construct a matched 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} and show that even standard cold-start evaluation is regime-dependent.
    \item We establish promoted temporal/provenance benchmarks on patent and non-patent BindingDB slices and show that their ranking can differ sharply from the synthetic panel.
    \item We expand the model panel beyond pooled-LM baselines with a learner-control \texttt{RF} system and a graph-family \texttt{GraphDTA-style} probe, showing that benchmark sensitivity is not confined to one architecture family.
    \item We provide year-band and overlap-bucket diagnoses, matched support on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS}, and reusable split manifests and evaluator resources to make the benchmark suite easy to inspect and reuse.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of the paper first defines the benchmark setup, then reports the synthetic and temporal comparisons, and finally discusses why benchmark-qualified reporting is necessary for DTI generalization claims.
