Drug-target interaction (DTI) papers often use synthetic cold-start splits as proxies for generalization, but it remains unclear whether conclusions drawn from these splits transfer to real distribution shifts. We present a benchmark-first study showing that model rankings are highly sensitive to how the shift is defined. Using a compact external panel consisting of a plain base encoder and two recent literature baselines, \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}, we first build a 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}. Even within this single dataset, rankings are heterogeneous: \texttt{HyperPCM} is strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start} by mean \AUPRC{}, while \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target}. We then evaluate the same panel on a real temporal/provenance benchmark, \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, where the winner changes again. Over 3 seeds, \texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the strongest model at $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ \AUPRC{}, ahead of \texttt{base} ($0.7772 \pm 0.0086$) and \texttt{HyperPCM} ($0.7753 \pm 0.0050$). An earlier alternative patent cutoff preserves the same \texttt{DTI-LM > base} ordering, and paired bootstrap supports the main temporal \AUPRC{} delta. A year-band and overlap-bucket diagnosis further shows that temporal OOD is structured rather than monolithic: different models dominate different temporal slices and overlap strata. We package the split manifests, benchmark resources, and standalone evaluator for reuse. Within the studied model panel and benchmarks, these results show that synthetic cold splits and real OOD shifts are not interchangeable evaluation settings for DTI.
