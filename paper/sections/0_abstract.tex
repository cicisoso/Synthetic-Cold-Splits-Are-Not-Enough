Drug-target interaction (DTI) papers often use synthetic cold-start splits as proxies for generalization, but it remains unclear whether conclusions drawn from these splits transfer to real distribution shifts. We present a benchmark-first case study showing that, within a studied three-model panel, rankings are sensitive to how the shift is defined. Using a compact external panel consisting of a fully specified fixed-feature \texttt{base} encoder and two recent literature baselines, \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}, we first build a 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}. Even within this single dataset, rankings are heterogeneous: \texttt{HyperPCM} is strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start} by mean \AUPRC{}, while \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target}. We then evaluate the same panel on a real temporal/provenance benchmark, \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, where the winner changes again. Over 3 seeds, \texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the strongest model at $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ \AUPRC{}, ahead of \texttt{base} ($0.7772 \pm 0.0086$) and \texttt{HyperPCM} ($0.7753 \pm 0.0050$). This margin is statistically positive but small in absolute magnitude, so we interpret it as a panel-level ranking change rather than a large deployment gap. An earlier alternative patent cutoff preserves the same \texttt{DTI-LM > base} ordering. A pooled year-band and overlap-bucket diagnosis further shows that temporal/provenance OOD is structured rather than monolithic: different models dominate different temporal slices and overlap strata. We package the split manifests, benchmark resources, and standalone evaluator for reuse. Within the studied model panel and benchmarks, these results show that synthetic cold splits and real OOD shifts should not be treated as interchangeable evaluation settings for DTI.
