Reliable validation of drug-target interaction (DTI) models remains challenging because synthetic cold-start splits are often used as surrogates for prospective generalization. Here, we examine how benchmark design alters model ranking in DTI prediction. We evaluated a five-model comparison panel comprising a descriptor-based MLP reference model, a Random Forest trained on the same Morgan fingerprint/protein k-mer descriptor set, two pooled language-model baselines (\texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}), and a graph-based \texttt{GraphDTA-style} baseline. Across three synthetic \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} splits, the highest-ranked model depended on the split definition: the Random Forest performed best on \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start}, whereas the descriptor-based MLP remained strongest on \texttt{unseen\_target}. We then evaluated temporal/provenance benchmarks derived from patent and non-patent BindingDB subsets and observed further ranking changes. Additional scaffold-restricted and overlap-restricted analyses showed that conclusions within the temporal family were themselves sensitive to overlap policy. Year-band and overlap-bucket analyses further indicated that temporal out-of-distribution behavior is structured rather than monolithic. To support reproducible evaluation, we release split manifests, benchmark tables, and a standalone evaluator. These results show that synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks are not interchangeable validation settings for DTI prediction, and that benchmark-qualified reporting is necessary when making model generalization claims.
