\section{Results}

\subsection{Synthetic cold splits are already regime-dependent}

We begin with the \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} synthetic reference panel. Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows the main pattern: even within a single dataset, model rankings are not stable across split definitions. On \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the plain \texttt{base} model achieves $0.5509 \pm 0.0900$ \AUPRC{}, while \texttt{RAICD} drops to $0.5228 \pm 0.0828$. On \texttt{blind\_start}, the pattern reverses, and \texttt{RAICD} improves over \texttt{base} from $0.3737 \pm 0.0229$ to $0.4024 \pm 0.0508$. On \texttt{unseen\_target}, \texttt{FTM sparse} becomes the strongest model, improving \AUPRC{} from $0.5224 \pm 0.0602$ to $0.5439 \pm 0.0791$. This synthetic target-cold gain is also supported by paired bootstrap (\texttt{FTM - base} $= +0.0214$, 95\% CI $[+0.0143, +0.0286]$), so the synthetic panel's winner claims are not based only on seed means.

These results matter because they show that ``synthetic cold-start DTI'' is already not a single evaluation condition. Different synthetic regimes reward different modeling choices, so it is unsafe to generalize from one split to another without explicit evidence.

\subsection{Real temporal shift reverses the retrieval conclusion and favors a different strong baseline}

We next evaluate the same internal panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, our primary real-OOD benchmark. Over three seeds, the plain \texttt{base} model reaches $0.7772 \pm 0.0086$ \AUPRC{} and $0.7223 \pm 0.0109$ \AUROC{}. \texttt{RAICD} falls to $0.7692 \pm 0.0018$ \AUPRC{} and $0.7032 \pm 0.0010$ \AUROC{}, while \texttt{FTM sparse} reaches $0.7721 \pm 0.0025$ \AUPRC{} and $0.7059 \pm 0.0037$ \AUROC{}. Figure~\ref{fig:delta-summary} compresses the main benchmark story into within-benchmark \AUPRC{} deltas relative to \texttt{base}: the same retrieval bias that wins on synthetic \texttt{blind\_start} becomes negative on patent temporal OOD. Paired bootstrap intervals support both patent losses relative to \texttt{base}: \texttt{RAICD - base} $= -0.0080$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0101, -0.0059]$, and \texttt{FTM - base} $= -0.0050$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0072, -0.0029]$.

This is the central ranking reversal in the paper. Retrieval is the winning inductive bias on synthetic \texttt{blind\_start}, but it loses clearly on real temporal/provenance OOD. The synthetic \texttt{blind\_start} gain is itself stable under paired bootstrap (\texttt{RAICD - base} $= +0.0288$, 95\% CI $[+0.0145, +0.0431]$), which makes the reversal more consequential rather than easier to dismiss. \texttt{FTM sparse} remains closer to \texttt{base} than \texttt{RAICD}, but still underperforms the plain model on both \AUPRC{} and \AUROC{}. When the model panel is expanded, the promoted pooled-LM baseline \texttt{DTI-LM} reaches $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ \AUPRC{} and $0.7362 \pm 0.0083$ \AUROC{} on the same patent benchmark, exceeding the compact internal \texttt{base}. This gain is also supported by paired bootstrap: \texttt{DTI-LM - base} $= +0.0054$ with 95\% CI $[+0.0028, +0.0079]$. The alternative-cutoff benchmark tells the same story over matched three-seed evaluation: on \texttt{patent\_temporal\_v2017}, \texttt{DTI-LM} reaches $0.7134 \pm 0.0027 / 0.6754 \pm 0.0039$, versus $0.7044 \pm 0.0058 / 0.6624 \pm 0.0079$ for \texttt{base}. The broader lesson is therefore not that one internal variant wins everywhere, but that both benchmark axis and model family change the apparent ranking.

\subsection{Temporal shift is structured across year bands and overlap levels}

To help explain why the temporal benchmark changes the ranking, we analyze the patent results by year and by overlap bucket. Table~\ref{tab:patent-diagnosis-compact} makes the primary \AUPRC{} slices explicit, while Figure~\ref{fig:patent-diagnosis} visualizes the same structure. In the large 2019 slice, \texttt{base} and \texttt{FTM} are nearly tied in \AUPRC{} ($0.7626$ vs.\ $0.7624$), but \texttt{base} maintains a clearer \AUROC{} advantage ($0.7086$ vs.\ $0.6992$). In 2020, the gap becomes much larger: \texttt{base} reaches $0.8715 / 0.8135$, while \texttt{RAICD} and \texttt{FTM} drop to $0.8395 / 0.7596$ and $0.8358 / 0.7534$, respectively. The 2021 slice is small, but follows the same ordering.

The overlap-bucket analysis reveals another useful pattern. In the lowest-overlap bucket, \texttt{base} is the strongest model by \AUPRC{}, and both \texttt{RAICD} and \texttt{FTM} remain slightly weaker. In the middle-overlap bucket, \texttt{RAICD} and \texttt{FTM} gain a modest \AUPRC{} advantage over \texttt{base}, but still lose on \AUROC{}. In the highest-overlap bucket, \texttt{base} again dominates on both metrics. Together, these results are consistent with the view that temporal OOD is not just a colder version of synthetic cold-start. Instead, it changes which subpopulations dominate the aggregate result, and therefore changes the model ranking.

\subsection{Supporting target-cold OOD benchmarks still favor the plain base model}

We treat \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} as supporting real-OOD benchmarks rather than primary headline axes. Both are informative because they test whether the synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target} advantage of \texttt{FTM sparse} transfers beyond \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}. Over matched three-seed evaluation, it does not. On \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6934 \pm 0.0257 / 0.7192 \pm 0.0068$, compared with $0.6685 \pm 0.0223 / 0.6919 \pm 0.0052$ for \texttt{RAICD} and $0.6760 \pm 0.0333 / 0.7007 \pm 0.0110$ for \texttt{FTM sparse}. On \texttt{DAVIS}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.4097 \pm 0.0275 / 0.8613 \pm 0.0153$, compared with $0.3839 \pm 0.0134 / 0.8565 \pm 0.0154$ for \texttt{RAICD} and $0.3787 \pm 0.0312 / 0.8577 \pm 0.0101$ for \texttt{FTM sparse}.

The strongest recent baseline does not overturn this support-panel story. In seed0 screens, \texttt{DTI-LM} also remains below \texttt{base} on both benchmarks: $0.6410 / 0.6946$ versus $0.6574 / 0.7103$ on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, and $0.3478 / 0.8674$ versus $0.4009 / 0.8817$ on \texttt{DAVIS}. The internal support-panel losses are also stable under paired bootstrap: on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{RAICD - base} $= -0.0249$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0271, -0.0226]$ and \texttt{FTM - base} $= -0.0174$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0197, -0.0150]$; on \texttt{DAVIS}, \texttt{RAICD - base} $= -0.0259$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0375, -0.0127]$ and \texttt{FTM - base} $= -0.0310$ with 95\% CI $[-0.0434, -0.0177]$. These auxiliary negatives are therefore consistent with the main claim of the paper, but they should not carry the same argumentative weight as the patent benchmark because they do not add a cleaner ranking-reversal axis than temporal/provenance shift.
