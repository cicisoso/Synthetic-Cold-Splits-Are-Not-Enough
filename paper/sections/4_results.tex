\section{Results}

\subsection{Synthetic cold splits are already regime-dependent}

We begin with the \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} synthetic reference panel. Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows the main pattern: even within a single dataset, model rankings are not stable across split definitions. On \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the highest mean \AUPRC{} is obtained by \texttt{RF}, which reaches $0.7339 \pm 0.0051$ versus $0.5509 \pm 0.0900$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.5694 \pm 0.0473$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ $0.5004 \pm 0.0341$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ and $0.5626 \pm 0.0172$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} is strictly positive, and \texttt{RF} also remains above \texttt{HyperPCM} under paired bootstrap. On \texttt{blind\_start}, \texttt{RF} again reaches the highest mean \AUPRC{} at $0.4512 \pm 0.0373$, compared with $0.3737 \pm 0.0229$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} remains strictly positive. On \texttt{unseen\_target}, however, the synthetic target-cold story is more stable. \texttt{base} remains strongest by mean \AUPRC{} at $0.5224 \pm 0.0602$, while \texttt{RF} is close but slightly lower at $0.5183 \pm 0.0612$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero.

These results show that ``synthetic cold-start DTI'' is already not a single validation condition. Different synthetic regimes reward different model families, and the five-model comparison panel shows that this is not merely a pooled-language-model phenomenon. A Random Forest using the same descriptor set as \texttt{base} becomes strongest on two synthetic axes, while the graph-based baseline remains competitive but does not dominate. Additional similarity and scaffold analyses sharpen this point further: on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the held-out compounds still have median nearest-train Tanimoto $0.7732$ and Murcko scaffold match rate $0.6204$. When we replace entity holdout with a stricter scaffold split, the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge is no longer supported. Over three seeds on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd / scaffold\_drug}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6069 \pm 0.0161 / 0.8351 \pm 0.0037$, while \texttt{HyperPCM} reaches $0.5983 \pm 0.0368 / 0.8155 \pm 0.0204$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{HyperPCM - base} crosses zero (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:robustness-probes} and \ref{tab:bootstrap-summary}). The synthetic panel therefore remains useful, but both its drug-cold ordering and its apparent best learner are sensitive to how chemically strict the split actually is.

\subsection{Primary temporal/provenance benchmarks alter the ranking again}

We next evaluate the same five-model comparison panel on the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks. On \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, \texttt{RF} attains the highest mean \AUPRC{}, reaching $0.8234 \pm 0.0012$, compared with $0.7772 \pm 0.0086$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.7753 \pm 0.0050$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ and $0.7726 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. The paired bootstrap intervals for \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM} remain strictly positive. The methodological point, however, is not simply that \texttt{RF} is strongest on this benchmark. It is that the ranking differs from the synthetic reference panel and therefore depends on benchmark semantics.

A second temporal analysis strengthens this reading further. On the non-patent benchmark built directly from the official BindingDB release, \texttt{RF} again attains the highest mean \AUPRC{} over matched 3-seed evaluation, reaching $0.7201 \pm 0.0012 / 0.7627 \pm 0.0020$, versus $0.6531 \pm 0.0066 / 0.7644 \pm 0.0041$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.6369 \pm 0.0173 / 0.7333 \pm 0.0081$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and $0.6635 \pm 0.0142 / 0.7334 \pm 0.0096$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. Here too, the paired bootstrap intervals stay strictly positive for both \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM}. Together, the patent and non-patent analyses show that temporal/provenance evaluation does not preserve the ordering seen on the synthetic panel.

At the same time, two controlled patent variants show that this temporal ordering is not immutable once overlap is constrained more aggressively. In the pair-novel variant (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_pair\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the strongest \AUPRC{} model at $0.7491 \pm 0.0076$, ahead of \texttt{RF} at $0.7393 \pm 0.0015$ and \texttt{base} at $0.7378 \pm 0.0107$. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} is strictly positive, while \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero. In the drug-novel variant (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_drug\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} again remains strongest by \AUPRC{} at $0.7402 \pm 0.0077$, versus $0.7356 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{RF}$,$ with a paired interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} that also stays positive. These additional robustness analyses make the temporal conclusion more precise rather than weaker: the patent benchmark is sensitive not only to benchmark family but also to how much entity or pair recurrence is allowed inside a nominally temporal split.

This is the central result. The synthetic panel does not produce a single best model, and the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks do not preserve the same ordering either. Instead, both benchmark axis and model family change the apparent ranking. The alternative-cutoff benchmark remains useful because it shows that temporal ordering is not an artifact of a single year boundary, but the five-model comparison panel and the controlled patent variants make the broader point more strongly: the apparent best system can change not only across benchmarks but also across overlap policies inside the same temporal family.

\subsection{Temporal shift is structured across year bands and overlap levels}

To help explain why the temporal/provenance benchmark changes the ranking, we analyze the patent results by year band and by overlap bucket. Table~\ref{tab:patent-diagnosis-compact} makes the primary \AUPRC{} slices explicit, while Figure~\ref{fig:patent-diagnosis} visualizes the same structure. To avoid over-reading the tiny 2021 cell, we pool 2020 and 2021 into a single recent stratum. Within the five-model comparison panel, \texttt{RF} is strongest in both the large 2019 slice and the pooled 2020--2021 slice, so the year-band view does not support a single uniform explanation based on one model family alone.

The overlap-bucket analysis reveals another useful pattern. In the lowest-overlap bucket, \texttt{DTI-LM} remains the strongest system, but \texttt{RF} becomes strongest in the middle- and high-overlap buckets and therefore dominates the aggregate temporal benchmark. The structured-shift diagnosis is therefore reinforced: temporal OOD is not just a colder version of synthetic cold-start, because different overlap strata continue to favor different model families. The benchmark outcome depends on both the shift axis and the modeling family, not only on whether a model uses pooled language-model features.

\subsection{Supporting target-cold OOD benchmarks still favor the plain base model}

We treat \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} as supporting real-OOD benchmarks rather than primary headline axes. Under matched three-seed evaluation, neither pooled language-model baseline overturns the support-panel story. On \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6934 \pm 0.0257 / 0.7192 \pm 0.0068$, compared with $0.6648 \pm 0.0177 / 0.6936 \pm 0.0044$ for \texttt{DTI-LM} and $0.6515 \pm 0.0301 / 0.6757 \pm 0.0211$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}. On \texttt{DAVIS}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.4097 \pm 0.0275 / 0.8613 \pm 0.0153$, compared with $0.4090 \pm 0.0570 / 0.8718 \pm 0.0152$ for \texttt{DTI-LM} and $0.3840 \pm 0.0269 / 0.8610 \pm 0.0245$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}.

The \texttt{DAVIS} result is the closest supporting-panel case: \texttt{DTI-LM} is effectively tied with \texttt{base} on mean \AUPRC{} and slightly higher on mean \AUROC{}, but it still does not create a clean ordering separation of the kind seen on the patent benchmark. \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} is more decisive, with \texttt{base} ahead of both external baselines on both metrics. These auxiliary results are therefore consistent with the main claim of the paper, but they do not carry the same argumentative weight as the patent benchmark because they do not add a cleaner shift axis than temporal/provenance OOD.
