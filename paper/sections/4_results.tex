\section{Results}

\subsection{Synthetic cold splits are already regime-dependent}

We begin with the \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} synthetic reference panel. Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows the main pattern: even within a single dataset, model rankings are not stable across split definitions, and the ranking changes further once the learner-control and graph-family additions are made. On \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the strongest model is no longer \texttt{HyperPCM} but \texttt{RF}, which reaches $0.7339 \pm 0.0051$ versus $0.5509 \pm 0.0900$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.5694 \pm 0.0473$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ $0.5004 \pm 0.0341$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ and $0.5626 \pm 0.0172$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} is strictly positive, and \texttt{RF} also remains above \texttt{HyperPCM} under paired bootstrap. On \texttt{blind\_start}, the earlier ``no reliable winner'' result disappears once \texttt{RF} is added: \texttt{RF} reaches $0.4512 \pm 0.0373$, compared with $0.3737 \pm 0.0229$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} remains strictly positive. On \texttt{unseen\_target}, however, the synthetic target-cold story is more stable. \texttt{base} remains strongest by mean \AUPRC{} at $0.5224 \pm 0.0602$, while \texttt{RF} is close but slightly lower at $0.5183 \pm 0.0612$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero.

These results matter because they show that ``synthetic cold-start DTI'' is already not a single evaluation condition. Different synthetic regimes reward different model families, and the expanded panel shows that this is not merely a pooled-LM phenomenon. A classical learner on exactly the same handcrafted drug/target representation as \texttt{base} can become the strongest model on two synthetic axes, while the graph-family probe remains competitive but does not dominate. The review-driven similarity diagnosis sharpens this point further: on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the held-out compounds still have median nearest-train Tanimoto $0.7732$ and Murcko scaffold match rate $0.6204$. When we replace entity holdout with a stricter scaffold split, the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge is no longer supported. Over three seeds on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd / scaffold\_drug}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6069 \pm 0.0161 / 0.8351 \pm 0.0037$, while \texttt{HyperPCM} reaches $0.5983 \pm 0.0368 / 0.8155 \pm 0.0204$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{HyperPCM - base} crosses zero (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:robustness-probes} and \ref{tab:bootstrap-summary}). The synthetic panel therefore remains useful, but both its drug-cold ordering and its apparent best learner are sensitive to how chemically strict the split actually is.

\subsection{Real temporal shift favors a different learner again}

We next evaluate the expanded panel on the promoted temporal benchmarks. Once \texttt{RF} is added, the real-OOD picture changes again. On \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, \texttt{RF} becomes the strongest model by mean \AUPRC{}, reaching $0.8234 \pm 0.0012$, compared with $0.7772 \pm 0.0086$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.7753 \pm 0.0050$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ and $0.7726 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. Figure~\ref{fig:delta-summary} therefore needs a stronger reading than in the earlier four-model draft: synthetic cold splits favor different systems, but the promoted temporal benchmarks now favor a classical learner-control baseline rather than a pooled-LM system. This advantage is statistically strong. Paired bootstrap gives \texttt{RF - base} $= +0.0463$ with 95\% CI $[+0.0441, +0.0484]$ and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM} $= +0.0409$ with 95\% CI $[+0.0384, +0.0433]$.

A second temporal robustness probe strengthens this reading further. On the non-patent benchmark built directly from the official BindingDB release, \texttt{RF} again becomes the strongest model over matched 3-seed evaluation, reaching $0.7201 \pm 0.0012 / 0.7627 \pm 0.0020$, versus $0.6531 \pm 0.0066 / 0.7644 \pm 0.0041$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.6369 \pm 0.0173 / 0.7333 \pm 0.0081$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and $0.6635 \pm 0.0142 / 0.7334 \pm 0.0096$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. Here too, the paired bootstrap intervals stay strictly positive for both \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM}. These additions change the scientific read of the temporal axis. The earlier conclusion that ``pooled-LM features are best on temporal OOD'' no longer survives the expanded panel. The stronger and more defensible conclusion is that temporal benchmarks do not preserve the ranking seen on synthetic panels, and that a classical learner on fixed handcrafted inputs can dominate both synthetic and temporal regimes.

At the same time, two controlled patent variants show that this temporal ordering is not immutable once overlap is constrained more aggressively. When we remove recurrent train pairs (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_pair\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the strongest \AUPRC{} model at $0.7491 \pm 0.0076$, ahead of \texttt{RF} at $0.7393 \pm 0.0015$ and \texttt{base} at $0.7378 \pm 0.0107$. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} is strictly positive, while \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero. When we remove seen-drug overlap (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_drug\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} again remains strongest by \AUPRC{} at $0.7402 \pm 0.0077$, versus $0.7356 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{RF}$,$ with a paired interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} that also stays positive. These review-driven controls make the temporal conclusion more disciplined rather than weaker: the promoted patent axis is sensitive not only to benchmark family but also to how much entity or pair recurrence is allowed inside a nominally temporal split.

This is the central revised result. The synthetic panel does not produce a single best model, and the promoted temporal benchmarks do not preserve the smaller panel's earlier ordering either. Instead, both benchmark axis and model family change the apparent ranking within the studied panel. The alternative-cutoff benchmark from the earlier revision remains useful because it still shows that temporal ordering is not an artifact of a single year boundary, but the expanded five-model panel and the controlled patent variants now make the broader point more strongly: the apparent best system can change not only across benchmarks but also across overlap policies inside the same temporal family.

\subsection{Temporal shift is structured across year bands and overlap levels}

To help explain why the temporal/provenance benchmark changes the ranking, we analyze the patent results by year band and by overlap bucket. Table~\ref{tab:patent-diagnosis-compact} makes the primary \AUPRC{} slices explicit, while Figure~\ref{fig:patent-diagnosis} visualizes the same structure. To avoid over-reading the tiny 2021 cell, we pool 2020 and 2021 into a single recent stratum. Under the expanded panel, \texttt{RF} is strongest in both the large 2019 slice and the pooled 2020--2021 slice, so the year-band view no longer supports a simple ``LM wins temporal OOD'' interpretation. Instead, it shows that the aggregate temporal ranking is not tied to one model family alone.

The overlap-bucket analysis reveals another useful pattern. In the lowest-overlap bucket, \texttt{DTI-LM} still remains the strongest system, but \texttt{RF} becomes strongest in the middle- and high-overlap buckets and therefore dominates the aggregate temporal benchmark. The expanded panel thus strengthens, rather than weakens, the structured-shift diagnosis: temporal OOD is not just a colder version of synthetic cold-start, because different overlap strata continue to favor different model families. The benchmark outcome depends on both the shift axis and the modeling family, not only on whether a model uses pooled LM features.

\subsection{Supporting target-cold OOD benchmarks still favor the plain base model}

We treat \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} as supporting real-OOD benchmarks rather than primary headline axes. Under matched three-seed evaluation, neither promoted external baseline overturns the support-panel story. On \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6934 \pm 0.0257 / 0.7192 \pm 0.0068$, compared with $0.6648 \pm 0.0177 / 0.6936 \pm 0.0044$ for \texttt{DTI-LM} and $0.6515 \pm 0.0301 / 0.6757 \pm 0.0211$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}. On \texttt{DAVIS}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.4097 \pm 0.0275 / 0.8613 \pm 0.0153$, compared with $0.4090 \pm 0.0570 / 0.8718 \pm 0.0152$ for \texttt{DTI-LM} and $0.3840 \pm 0.0269 / 0.8610 \pm 0.0245$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}.

The \texttt{DAVIS} result is the closest supporting-panel case: \texttt{DTI-LM} is effectively tied with \texttt{base} on mean \AUPRC{} and slightly higher on mean \AUROC{}, but it still does not create a clean ordering separation of the kind seen on the promoted patent benchmark. \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} is more decisive, with \texttt{base} ahead of both external baselines on both metrics. These auxiliary results are therefore consistent with the main claim of the paper, but they do not carry the same argumentative weight as the patent benchmark because they do not add a cleaner shift axis than temporal/provenance OOD.
