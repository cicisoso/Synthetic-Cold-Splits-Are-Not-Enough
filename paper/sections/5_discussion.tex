\section{Discussion}

The main message of this study is that, within the studied three-model panel, DTI evaluation is benchmark-sensitive in a way that current reporting practice often obscures. A synthetic split can still be useful, but it should not be treated as a universal proxy for real OOD generalization. Even within this compact panel, the sign of a method's apparent gain can change when the benchmark changes.

An important design choice in this paper is the use of a compact external model panel. A larger model zoo could be added later, but it is not necessary to establish the narrower claim studied here. The winner change already appears with three representative systems, and the shift-axis diagnosis helps explain where that winner change comes from. At the same time, the patent margin is small in absolute \AUPRC{}, so the present paper should not be read as claiming a large practical deployment gap between \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{base}. The safer interpretation is that a benchmark-qualified ranking reversal exists within this panel.

The paper also has clear limitations. Although the support benchmarks \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} remain useful counterexamples, they are still supporting rather than co-equal headline axes. The expanded model family is stronger than the original in-repo probe panel, but it is still partial rather than exhaustive: \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM} do not cover the full space of recent DTI architectures. We now add two targeted robustness probes, and both make the manuscript more disciplined. The non-patent temporal benchmark shows that the \texttt{DTI-LM} edge is not confined to patent provenance alone, while the scaffold-drug probe shows that the original synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} split was chemically permissive enough that the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge does not survive a stricter holdout. Even with these additions, the patent benchmark is still temporal/provenance OOD rather than a pure time-only shift, so the current paper should not claim that time alone explains the observed ranking change. We leave a mechanistic explanation for why pooled-LM features help more on temporal panels to future work, rather than claiming one from the current evidence alone. We also do not evaluate downstream biological decision quality. This paper should therefore be read as a well-supported benchmark case study rather than an exhaustive census of all real-OOD DTI settings. These limitations should be stated plainly, but they do not weaken the more disciplined central finding that synthetic and real-OOD rankings can disagree in the tested panel.
