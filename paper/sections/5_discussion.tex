\section{Discussion}

The main message of this study is that DTI evaluation is benchmark-sensitive in a way that current reporting practice often obscures. A synthetic split can still be useful, but it should not be treated as a universal proxy for real OOD generalization. Within the studied panel and benchmarks, even the sign of a method's apparent gain can change when the benchmark changes.

An important design choice in this paper is the use of a compact external model panel. A larger model zoo could be added later, but it is not necessary to establish the central claim studied here. The winner change already appears with three representative systems, and the shift-axis diagnosis helps explain why that winner change occurs. In this sense, benchmark design itself is the main object of study.

The paper also has clear limitations. Although the support benchmarks \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} remain useful counterexamples, they are still supporting rather than co-equal headline axes. The expanded model family is stronger than the original in-repo probe panel, but it is still partial rather than exhaustive: \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM} do not cover the full space of recent DTI architectures. We add stronger statistical support to the comparisons used for headline conclusions, but not to every descriptive cell in the result tables. We also do not evaluate downstream biological decision quality. This paper should therefore be read as a well-supported benchmark case study rather than an exhaustive census of all real-OOD DTI settings. These limitations should be stated plainly, but they do not weaken the more disciplined central finding that synthetic and real-OOD rankings can disagree in the tested panel.
