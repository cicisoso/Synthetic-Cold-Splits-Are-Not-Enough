\abstract{Reliable validation of drug-target interaction (DTI) models remains challenging because synthetic cold-start splits are often used as surrogates for prospective generalization. Here, we examine how benchmark design alters model ranking in DTI prediction. We evaluated a five-model comparison panel. It comprises a descriptor-based MLP reference model, a Random Forest trained on the same Morgan fingerprint/protein k-mer descriptor set, two pooled language-model baselines (\texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}), and a graph-based \texttt{GraphDTA-style} baseline. Across three synthetic \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} splits, the highest-ranked model depended on the split definition: the Random Forest performed best on \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start}, whereas the descriptor-based MLP remained strongest on \texttt{unseen\_target}. We then evaluated temporal/provenance benchmarks derived from patent and non-patent BindingDB subsets and observed further ranking changes. A scaffold-restricted synthetic control sharpened the synthetic-side interpretation, while overlap-restricted patent variants showed that conclusions within the temporal family were themselves sensitive to overlap policy. Year-band and overlap-bucket analyses further indicated that temporal/provenance out-of-distribution behavior is structured rather than monolithic. To support reproducible evaluation, we release split manifests, benchmark tables, and a standalone evaluator. These results show that synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks are not interchangeable validation settings for DTI prediction, and that benchmark-qualified reporting is necessary when making model generalization claims.}

\section{Introduction}

Predicting drug-target interactions for unseen molecules or proteins is a central goal in computational drug discovery. Because truly prospective evaluation is difficult and expensive, many recent studies report held-out drug, target, or blind splits on datasets derived from BindingDB or DAVIS as proxies for inductive generalization \citep{liu2007bindingdb,davis2011kinase,ahmed2024dtilm,luo2024druglamp,ouyang2024pmmr,liu2025spdti,yu2025gsdti,zhao2025coldstartcpi}. These protocols are useful, but they also encourage a strong implicit assumption: if a method performs well on one cold-start split, then it is broadly robust to the kinds of shift encountered in deployment.

That assumption is weaker than it appears. Different split definitions remove different forms of overlap, and they may favor different model families. A pooled-LM baseline that looks strong under one synthetic exclusion rule may no longer be the most reliable option once the benchmark shifts in time, provenance, or assay composition. If these ranking changes occur even within a compact studied panel, benchmark design is not a technical footnote. It becomes part of the scientific claim.

This paper studies that question directly. Rather than proposing another DTI architecture, we examine whether model ranking is preserved across synthetic cold-start regimes and temporal/provenance shifts. We use a five-model comparison panel consisting of a descriptor-based MLP reference model (\texttt{base}), a Random Forest trained on the same Morgan fingerprint/protein k-mer descriptor set, two pooled language-model baselines (\texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}), and a graph-based \texttt{GraphDTA-style} baseline. Even on the synthetic reference panel, rankings are already heterogeneous: \texttt{RF} is strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} and \texttt{blind\_start}, while \texttt{base} remains strongest on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_target}.

We then contrast this synthetic picture with primary temporal/provenance benchmarks built from BindingDB patent and non-patent time-based splits. The resulting ordering changes again. The synthetic panel does not point to a single dominant system, and the temporal family introduces additional sensitivity to overlap policy within the same benchmark family. Paired bootstrap confidence intervals support the main temporal comparisons, but the broader claim is about benchmark-qualified ranking rather than any universal winner: benchmark definition materially changes the apparent ordering and therefore changes the conclusion a practitioner would draw about model generalization.

To move beyond leaderboard-style reporting, we also diagnose the primary temporal/provenance benchmark by year band and overlap bucket. This analysis shows that temporal/provenance OOD is not simply ``harder cold-start.'' Instead, the gap is concentrated in specific year bands and overlap regimes, which helps explain why a method that looks strong under one split can lose under another. We further include matched 3-seed support on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} to test whether the same model family remains stable across shifted target-cold settings. Recent molecular benchmark studies have made analogous arguments about OOD sensitivity in adjacent tasks \citep{ektefaie2024generalizability,shen2025ddiben,delafuente2025inductive}; our contribution is to bring that perspective into DTI as a reproducible validation study with reusable split manifests and a standalone evaluator.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We construct a matched 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} and show that even standard cold-start evaluation is regime-dependent.
    \item We establish primary temporal/provenance benchmarks on patent and non-patent BindingDB slices and show that their ranking can differ sharply from the synthetic panel.
    \item We compare descriptor-based, pooled language-model, and graph-based baselines within one consistent five-model comparison panel, showing that benchmark sensitivity is not confined to one architecture family.
    \item We provide year-band and overlap-bucket diagnoses, matched support on \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS}, and reusable split manifests and evaluator resources to make the benchmark suite easy to inspect and reuse.
\end{enumerate}

For practical model selection in DTI, these results imply that a single synthetic split is not sufficient evidence for generalization. At minimum, one synthetic axis and one temporal/provenance-shifted axis should be reported when models are compared for compound--target prioritization or related downstream use. The rest of the paper first defines the benchmark setup, then reports the synthetic and temporal/provenance comparisons, and finally discusses the implications for DTI validation practice.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Synthetic cold-start evaluation in DTI.} Machine-learning models for DTI and affinity prediction are commonly evaluated on BindingDB- and DAVIS-derived random, cold-drug, cold-target, or blind splits \citep{liu2007bindingdb,davis2011kinase,ahmed2024dtilm,luo2024druglamp,ouyang2024pmmr,liu2025spdti,yu2025gsdti,zhao2025coldstartcpi}. These protocols are useful for controlled comparison, but they eliminate different forms of chemical, protein, and pair overlap and therefore do not represent a single validation setting. For this reason, results obtained on one synthetic split should not automatically be interpreted as evidence of general robustness.

\textbf{Validation design in chemoinformatics.} More broadly in chemoinformatics, validation design and test-set construction are known to influence apparent model performance. Reanalysis of large bioactivity benchmarks, increasingly challenging potency-prediction partitions, and transfer-learning studies for binary drug--target interactions have all shown that the relative standing of deep and classical models can change materially when the evaluation setting changes \citep{robinson2020validating,rodriguezperez2021evaluation,tanoori2021binding}. Our study follows this validation-oriented tradition by asking how split semantics alter conclusions in a DTI setting built around widely used BindingDB and DAVIS resources.

\textbf{Benchmark and resource studies under distribution shift.} Recent benchmark studies in molecular machine learning and related biomedical prediction problems have shown that scaffold, temporal, and domain-shifted evaluation can change model ranking and alter the practical interpretation of benchmark results \citep{ektefaie2024generalizability,shen2025ddiben,delafuente2025inductive}. This literature has shifted attention from purely i.i.d.\ comparison toward reusable benchmark resources that expose how conclusions depend on data partitioning. Our work extends that perspective to DTI by coupling multiple benchmark families with exported split manifests and a standalone evaluator.

\textbf{Positioning.} Prior DTI papers typically optimize performance within one benchmark design. In contrast, our contribution is a reproducible case study of ranking stability across synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks, together with overlap-sensitive robustness analyses and reusable evaluation resources. The emphasis is therefore on validation methodology and benchmark semantics rather than on proposing a new predictive architecture.

\section{Benchmark Setup}

Our goal is to test whether model ranking is preserved across synthetic cold-start regimes and real distribution shifts. We therefore use a five-model comparison panel. \texttt{base} is a descriptor-based MLP reference system built from RDKit Morgan fingerprints and hashed protein k-mer features. \texttt{RF} is a Random Forest baseline trained on the same descriptor set as \texttt{base}, which allows the effect of the learning algorithm to be separated from the effect of the representation. \texttt{DTI-LM} is a pooled ChemBERTa+ESM2 baseline aligned to recent literature, \texttt{HyperPCM} is a task-conditioned pooled-LM baseline, and \texttt{GraphDTA-style} is a graph-based baseline with a molecular GCN and protein CNN. Together these models span descriptor-based neural and classical learners, pooled language-model baselines, and a graph-based baseline. Because the anchor system is central to every ranking claim, Appendix Table~\ref{tab:base-spec} gives the exact \texttt{base} feature construction, architecture, parameter count, and training recipe.

We evaluate this comparison panel on two benchmark groups. The first is a synthetic reference panel based on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}, with three split definitions: \texttt{unseen\_drug}, \texttt{blind\_start}, and \texttt{unseen\_target}. The second comprises temporal/provenance and external-shift benchmarks. Our primary temporal/provenance analyses are two year-based BindingDB benchmarks: a patent split (\texttt{patent\_temporal}) and a non-patent split (\texttt{nonpatent\_temporal}). Both are evaluated over three seeds. We also include \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} as supporting shifted target-cold benchmarks that test whether the same model family remains stable across assay and dataset shifts. Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} summarizes the shift taxonomy and split statistics.

We report \AUPRC{} as the primary metric and \AUROC{} as a secondary metric. \AUPRC{} is emphasized because within each benchmark the central question is imbalanced ranking quality, and precision--recall behavior is the quantity most directly tied to the benchmark-sensitive model-selection claims made here. Because prevalence ranges from $0.0615$ on \texttt{DAVIS} to $0.5745$ on \texttt{patent\_temporal}, we do not interpret absolute \AUPRC{} values across benchmark families. Instead, \AUPRC{} is compared within benchmark, while \AUROC{} is retained as a prevalence-insensitive secondary check. For very low-prevalence settings such as \texttt{DAVIS}, enrichment-oriented metrics such as enrichment factor or BEDROC would add useful screening-specific context; we leave these to future benchmark extensions so that all panels in this study can still be compared under one consistent metric pair. We report matched three-seed results on the synthetic reference panel and on the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks. The supporting \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} panels are also reported with matched three-seed evaluation, but they are interpreted as supporting evidence rather than as co-equal headline axes. Paired bootstrap confidence intervals are used for the principal ranking comparisons. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap-summary} collects these intervals.

To make the benchmark reusable, each split is exported as a canonical resource bundle with manifests, split tables, cached identifiers, and a standalone CSV-based evaluator. This resource layer allows external predictions to be scored against the exact same test partitions used in the paper. In practical terms, an external user only needs a split bundle plus a prediction CSV keyed by \texttt{example\_id}; the reported metrics can then be reproduced with \texttt{python scripts/evaluate\_prediction\_csv.py --manifest manifest.json --predictions preds.csv}. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:resource-release} enumerates the released artifacts and how they are consumed by the evaluator.

The patent benchmark requires several setup choices that are documented explicitly. We use a local year-based split of \texttt{BindingDB\_patent}, with training years before the validation year, a held-out validation year, and a later temporal test set. Because this benchmark also changes provenance and assay composition, we describe it as temporal/provenance OOD rather than pure time shift. We also use a patent-specific affinity conversion rather than reusing the standard \texttt{pKd} transform, because the patent benchmark stores affinities in a different log-scale convention. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:patent-spec} summarizes the exact year boundaries, split counts, label construction, overlap policy, and seed semantics for both \texttt{patent\_temporal} and the alternative-cutoff \texttt{patent\_temporal\_v2017}. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:nonpatent-spec} provides the same information for the additional \texttt{BindingDB\_nonpatent\_Kd / nonpatent\_temporal} temporal analysis. BindingDB-derived panels are anchored in the BindingDB resource \citep{liu2007bindingdb}, while DAVIS provides an external kinase-focused support benchmark \citep{davis2011kinase}.

Finally, for diagnostic analysis, we use the saved overlap score from each test prediction to compute overlap buckets that summarize how strongly each test example resembles the training support. Concretely, \texttt{overlap\_score} is defined as $\min(s_d, s_t)$, where $s_d$ is the cosine similarity between the test drug's Morgan fingerprint and its nearest training-drug neighbor, and $s_t$ is the cosine similarity between the test target's hashed k-mer feature vector and its nearest training-target neighbor. We use the minimum to conservatively define overlap: a pair is high-overlap only when both its drug and target are well represented in the training set. We retain cosine for the released overlap score so that drug-side and target-side overlap share the same similarity scale inside this joint diagnostic; the separate synthetic similarity analysis still reports the standard Morgan-fingerprint Tanimoto and scaffold-overlap statistics. We report benchmark taxonomy and split statistics, including test-set size and prevalence, so that \AUPRC{} is interpreted within benchmark rather than across benchmark families.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Synthetic cold splits are already regime-dependent}

We begin with the \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} synthetic reference panel. Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows the main pattern: even within a single dataset, model rankings are not stable across split definitions. On \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the highest mean \AUPRC{} is obtained by \texttt{RF}, which reaches $0.7339 \pm 0.0051$ versus $0.5509 \pm 0.0900$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.5694 \pm 0.0473$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ $0.5004 \pm 0.0341$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ and $0.5626 \pm 0.0172$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} is strictly positive, and \texttt{RF} also remains above \texttt{HyperPCM} under paired bootstrap. On \texttt{blind\_start}, \texttt{RF} again reaches the highest mean \AUPRC{} at $0.4512 \pm 0.0373$, compared with $0.3910 \pm 0.0504$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}$,$ $0.3828 \pm 0.1024$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ and $0.3737 \pm 0.0229$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} remains strictly positive. On \texttt{unseen\_target}, however, the synthetic target-cold story is more stable. \texttt{base} remains strongest by mean \AUPRC{} at $0.5224 \pm 0.0602$, while \texttt{RF} is close but slightly lower at $0.5183 \pm 0.0612$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero.

These results show that ``synthetic cold-start DTI'' is already not a single validation condition. Different synthetic regimes reward different model families, and the five-model comparison panel shows that this is not merely a pooled-language-model phenomenon. A Random Forest using the same descriptor set as \texttt{base} becomes strongest on two synthetic axes, while the graph-based baseline remains competitive but does not dominate. Additional similarity and scaffold analyses sharpen this point further: on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, the held-out compounds still have median nearest-train Tanimoto $0.7732$ and Murcko scaffold match rate $0.6204$. When we replace entity holdout with a stricter scaffold split, the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge is no longer supported. Over three seeds on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd / scaffold\_drug}, \texttt{base} reaches $0.6069 \pm 0.0161 / 0.8351 \pm 0.0037$, while \texttt{HyperPCM} reaches $0.5983 \pm 0.0368 / 0.8155 \pm 0.0204$, and the paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{HyperPCM - base} crosses zero (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:robustness-probes} and \ref{tab:bootstrap-summary}). The synthetic panel therefore remains useful, but both its drug-cold ordering and its apparent best learner are sensitive to how chemically strict the split actually is.

\subsection{Primary temporal/provenance benchmarks alter the ranking again}

We next evaluate the same five-model comparison panel on the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks. On \texttt{BindingDB\_patent / patent\_temporal}, \texttt{RF} attains the highest mean \AUPRC{}, reaching $0.8234 \pm 0.0012$, compared with $0.7772 \pm 0.0086$ for \texttt{base}$,$ $0.7825 \pm 0.0078$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.7753 \pm 0.0050$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}$,$ and $0.7726 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}. The paired bootstrap intervals for \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM} remain strictly positive. The key observation is therefore not merely that \texttt{RF} wins this benchmark, but that the ranking differs from the synthetic reference panel and therefore depends on benchmark semantics.

A second temporal analysis strengthens this reading further. On the non-patent benchmark built directly from the official BindingDB release, \texttt{RF} again attains the highest mean \AUPRC{} over matched 3-seed evaluation, reaching $0.7201 \pm 0.0012 / 0.7627 \pm 0.0020$, versus $0.6635 \pm 0.0142 / 0.7334 \pm 0.0096$ for \texttt{GraphDTA-style}$,$ $0.6531 \pm 0.0066 / 0.7644 \pm 0.0041$ for \texttt{DTI-LM}$,$ $0.6369 \pm 0.0173 / 0.7333 \pm 0.0081$ for \texttt{base}$,$ and $0.6287 \pm 0.0041 / 0.7450 \pm 0.0088$ for \texttt{HyperPCM}. Here too, the paired bootstrap intervals stay strictly positive for both \texttt{RF - base} and \texttt{RF - DTI-LM}. Together, the patent and non-patent analyses show that temporal/provenance evaluation does not preserve the ordering seen on the synthetic panel.

At the same time, two controlled patent variants show that this temporal ordering is not immutable once overlap is constrained more aggressively. In the pair-novel variant (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_pair\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the strongest \AUPRC{} model at $0.7491 \pm 0.0076$, ahead of \texttt{RF} at $0.7393 \pm 0.0015$ and \texttt{base} at $0.7378 \pm 0.0107$. The paired bootstrap interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} is strictly positive, while \texttt{RF - base} crosses zero. In the drug-novel variant (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_drug\_novel}), \texttt{DTI-LM} again remains strongest by \AUPRC{} at $0.7402 \pm 0.0077$, versus $0.7356 \pm 0.0016$ for \texttt{RF}$,$ with a paired interval for \texttt{DTI-LM - RF} that also stays positive. These additional robustness analyses make the temporal conclusion more precise rather than weaker: the patent benchmark is sensitive not only to benchmark family but also to how much entity or pair recurrence is allowed inside a nominally temporal split. Figure~\ref{fig:delta-summary} is therefore a compact diagnostic reduction of Table~\ref{tab:main-results} rather than a second copy of the full five-model panel: it shows only deltas against \texttt{base}, so \texttt{base} itself defines the zero line, and it restricts the display to \texttt{RF}, \texttt{DTI-LM}, and \texttt{HyperPCM} as compact representatives of the descriptor-based, pooled-language-model, and hybrid comparison families.

This is the central result. The synthetic panel does not produce a single best model, and the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks do not preserve the same ordering either. Instead, both benchmark axis and model family change the apparent ranking. A narrower earlier-cutoff probe also points in the same direction: under \texttt{patent\_temporal\_v2017}, the matched 3-seed two-model ordering \texttt{DTI-LM > base} is preserved (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:patent-v2017-results}), which suggests that the patent-family sensitivity is not driven only by the 2018/2019 boundary. The five-model comparison panel and the controlled patent variants nevertheless carry the main argumentative weight, because they show more directly that the apparent best system can change not only across benchmarks but also across overlap policies inside the same temporal family.

\subsection{Patent temporal/provenance shift is structured across year bands and overlap levels}

To help explain why the temporal/provenance benchmark changes the ranking, we analyze the patent results by year band and by overlap bucket. Table~\ref{tab:patent-diagnosis-compact} and Figure~\ref{fig:patent-diagnosis} are intentionally compact: they focus on \texttt{base}, \texttt{RF}, and \texttt{DTI-LM}, because these three systems define the patent ranking story in the aggregate benchmark, the lowest-overlap bucket, and the controlled overlap variants. \texttt{HyperPCM} and \texttt{GraphDTA-style} remain reported in Table~\ref{tab:main-results}, but neither leads a year-band or overlap-bucket slice. To avoid over-reading the tiny 2021 cell, we pool 2020 and 2021 into a single recent stratum. Within the five-model comparison panel, \texttt{RF} is strongest in both the large 2019 slice and the pooled 2020--2021 slice, so the year-band view does not support a single uniform explanation based on one model family alone.

The overlap-bucket analysis reveals another useful pattern. In the lowest-overlap bucket, \texttt{DTI-LM} remains the strongest system, but \texttt{RF} becomes strongest in the middle- and high-overlap buckets and therefore dominates the aggregate temporal benchmark. The structured-shift diagnosis is therefore reinforced: temporal/provenance OOD is not just a colder version of synthetic cold-start, because different overlap strata continue to favor different model families. The benchmark outcome depends on both the shift axis and the modeling family, not only on whether a model uses pooled language-model features.

\subsection{Supporting target-cold OOD benchmarks remain heterogeneous}

We treat \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} as supporting real-OOD benchmarks rather than primary headline axes. Under matched three-seed evaluation, they extend the benchmark-sensitivity story rather than collapse it to a single winner. On \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{base} remains strongest at $0.6934 \pm 0.0257 / 0.7192 \pm 0.0068$, ahead of \texttt{DTI-LM} at $0.6648 \pm 0.0177 / 0.6936 \pm 0.0044$, \texttt{HyperPCM} at $0.6515 \pm 0.0301 / 0.6757 \pm 0.0211$, \texttt{RF} at $0.6328 \pm 0.0465 / 0.6581 \pm 0.0319$, and \texttt{GraphDTA-style} at $0.6280 \pm 0.0237 / 0.6550 \pm 0.0193$. On \texttt{DAVIS}, however, the ordering changes again: \texttt{RF} attains the highest mean \AUPRC{} at $0.4408 \pm 0.0325 / 0.8806 \pm 0.0128$, ahead of \texttt{base} at $0.4097 \pm 0.0275 / 0.8613 \pm 0.0153$, \texttt{DTI-LM} at $0.4090 \pm 0.0570 / 0.8718 \pm 0.0152$, \texttt{HyperPCM} at $0.3840 \pm 0.0269 / 0.8610 \pm 0.0245$, and \texttt{GraphDTA-style} at $0.3601 \pm 0.0502 / 0.8359 \pm 0.0292$.

These auxiliary results are therefore consistent with the main claim of the paper, but in a more informative way than the earlier pooled-LM-only view. \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki} and \texttt{DAVIS} do not recover a stable winner. Instead, they show that even among supporting target-cold OOD benchmarks, the apparent best family can differ by benchmark definition. They still carry less argumentative weight than the patent benchmark because they do not provide a cleaner shift axis than temporal/provenance OOD, but they reinforce the same benchmark-qualified interpretation of model generalization.

\section{Discussion}

The main message of this study is that DTI evaluation is benchmark-sensitive in a way that current reporting practice often obscures. A synthetic split can still be useful, but it should not be treated as a universal proxy for real OOD generalization. Within the five-model comparison panel studied here, the sign and magnitude of an apparent gain change when the benchmark definition changes, and temporal/provenance conclusions can change again when overlap policy is tightened within the same benchmark family.

For chemoinformatics practice, the implication is straightforward. A single synthetic cold split is not sufficient support for a generalization claim, and model selection can depend on whether evaluation emphasizes entity holdout, time/provenance shift, or overlap restriction. Three practical reporting recommendations follow from the present results. First, DTI studies should report at least one synthetic split together with one temporal/provenance-shifted benchmark when model generalization is discussed. Second, studies should disclose overlap characteristics, because entity or pair recurrence can materially change the apparent ranking even within one temporal benchmark family. Third, claims about the superiority of descriptor-based, pooled language-model, or graph-based models should be phrased in benchmark-qualified terms rather than as architecture-level general statements.

These recommendations are directly relevant to routine chemoinformatics workflows. When DTI models are used for compound--target prioritization, virtual screening support, or internal model selection, the preferred model can differ depending on whether validation resembles entity holdout or time/provenance shift. In that setting, benchmark diversity is not an aesthetic addition to the manuscript; it is part of the validation protocol needed to avoid overconfident selection decisions.

One empirical pattern also deserves a mechanism-level interpretation. \texttt{RF} is repeatedly strong on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, \texttt{blind\_start}, and both temporal/provenance panels, yet weaker on synthetic and external target-cold settings. Because \texttt{RF} and \texttt{base} use the same Morgan+k-mer descriptor set, this pattern is not about representation alone. It suggests that when held-out drugs remain close to seen scaffolds, or when temporal/provenance shift still preserves substantial drug-side support, tree ensembles can exploit local descriptor neighborhoods effectively without the additional smoothing assumptions imposed by the MLP. When the dominant challenge shifts toward protein-side extrapolation, as in \texttt{unseen\_target} and \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, the hashed k-mer representation appears to become the bottleneck and the earlier \texttt{RF} advantage disappears. We therefore interpret the recurrent \texttt{RF} gains as evidence of strong descriptor-space interpolation under drug-side support, not as a universal advantage of classical learners over neural models.

The study also has clear limitations. Although the five-model comparison panel spans descriptor-based neural and classical learners, pooled language-model baselines, and a graph-based baseline, it is still not exhaustive. Important recent architectures such as ColdstartCPI, DrugLAMP, and SP-DTI are not part of the present panel \citep{zhao2025coldstartcpi,luo2024druglamp,liu2025spdti}. Our conclusion should therefore be read as a methodological one rather than as a universal leaderboard claim: ranking instability is already visible in a compact but deliberately diverse panel, but a larger community benchmark would be needed to quantify how far this effect extends across the full modern DTI literature.

The patent benchmark also mixes temporal, provenance, and assay shifts, so it should be read as a structured temporal/provenance stress test rather than as a clean causal estimate of time shift alone. The year-band and overlap-bucket analyses show that behavior within this compound shift is heterogeneous, but they do not identify whether chronology, source curation, or assay composition is the dominant driver. The non-patent temporal benchmark partially reduces the provenance confound and preserves a ranking change, which argues against a patent-only artifact, but its test set is much smaller (7,174 versus 49,028 rows) and therefore cannot provide a one-to-one decomposition. A cleaner attribution would require larger source-matched temporal cohorts or prospective snapshots with harmonized assay composition.

Finally, the metric and uncertainty design should be read with their intended scope. \AUPRC{} is appropriate for within-benchmark model ranking under class imbalance, but it is not meant to support direct comparisons of absolute difficulty across benchmark families; for very low-prevalence settings such as \texttt{DAVIS}, enrichment-style metrics would be a useful screening-oriented extension. Likewise, three matched seeds are sufficient for the present validation study but not generous. We used them to balance compute against coverage of multiple benchmark families, and the paired bootstrap intervals provide example-level uncertainty for the principal ranking claims. Even so, high-variance rows such as synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug} indicate that larger seed counts or an explicit power analysis would strengthen future versions of the benchmark. We also do not evaluate prospective biological decision quality. These limitations do not weaken the central conclusion. They instead define its scope: benchmark definition changes model ranking in DTI, and even temporal/provenance conclusions depend on overlap policy.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a validation-oriented study of distribution shift in DTI prediction. Across a five-model comparison panel, a 3-seed synthetic reference panel on \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} showed that cold-start regimes are already heterogeneous, while temporal/provenance benchmarks built from patent and non-patent BindingDB subsets changed the ranking again. Additional scaffold-restricted analysis sharpened the synthetic-side interpretation, and overlap-restricted patent variants further showed that conclusions within the temporal family depend on overlap policy. Year-band and overlap-bucket analyses indicated that temporal/provenance OOD behavior is structured rather than monolithic. Overall, these results show that synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks are not interchangeable validation settings for DTI prediction. Future DTI studies should therefore report both synthetic and temporal/provenance evidence when making model-generalization claims and when selecting models for practical chemoinformatics workflows.
