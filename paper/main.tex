\abstract{Drug--target interaction (DTI) models are routinely validated on synthetic cold-start splits, yet practitioners deploy them under temporal and provenance shifts that these splits do not capture. Are these two evaluation settings interchangeable? We answer this question with a six-model panel spanning descriptor-based (MLP, Random Forest), language-model (DTI-LM, HyperPCM), graph-based (GraphDTA-style), and cross-attention (DrugBAN) architectures, evaluated across three synthetic \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} cold-start splits, two temporal/provenance benchmarks (patent and non-patent BindingDB), and two supporting target-cold benchmarks (\texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{DAVIS}). Three findings emerge. First, even within the synthetic panel the winner changes with the split definition. Second, temporal/provenance benchmarks produce a different ranking from the synthetic panel—and the non-patent temporal benchmark confirms that this is not a patent-provenance artifact. Third, overlap-restricted variants within the temporal family change the winner yet again, and an overlap-bucket diagnosis reveals that the temporal shift is structured rather than monolithic: different overlap strata favor different model families. These results demonstrate that synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks are non-interchangeable validation axes, and that benchmark-qualified reporting is necessary when making DTI generalization claims. We release reusable split manifests and a standalone evaluator to support reproducible comparison.}

\keywords{drug--target interaction, benchmark design, temporal validation, distribution shift, BindingDB}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Predicting drug--target interactions (DTIs) for novel compounds or proteins is a central task in computational drug discovery. Because prospective wet-lab validation is expensive and slow, the field relies heavily on retrospective held-out evaluation. The most common protocol is \emph{synthetic cold-start splitting}: compounds, targets, or both are held out at random so that the test set contains entities absent from training \citep{pahikkala2015toward,liu2007bindingdb,davis2011kinase}. Under this protocol, dozens of recent architectures—from language-model pooling \citep{ahmed2024dtilm} to graph neural networks \citep{nguyen2021graphdta} to contrastive pre-training \citep{singh2023conplex} and bilinear cross-attention \citep{bai2023drugban}—have reported competitive or state-of-the-art results, often on a single split of a single dataset. The implicit assumption is that strong performance on one cold-start split implies robust generalization to deployment conditions.

This assumption, however, has not been directly tested against temporal and provenance shifts in DTI. In adjacent domains, mounting evidence shows that evaluation design can change model rankings: time-split cross-validation better predicts prospective performance than random splits in QSAR \citep{sheridan2013timesplit}; scaffold splits and random splits can disagree on the best molecular property predictor \citep{yang2019chemprop}; and domain-shifted evaluation alters conclusions in drug--drug interaction and molecular generalizability studies \citep{ektefaie2024generalizability,shen2025ddiben}. Large-scale benchmarking efforts such as MoleculeNet \citep{wu2018moleculenet} and the Therapeutics Data Commons \citep{huang2022tdc} have further highlighted that no single model dominates across all tasks and splits. Yet in DTI specifically, the question of whether \emph{synthetic cold-start rankings transfer to temporal/provenance-shifted settings} has not been posed as a controlled experiment.

We address this gap. Rather than proposing a new architecture, we construct a systematic benchmark study that directly compares model rankings across synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance evaluation axes. We use a six-model panel deliberately spanning the major architectural families in current DTI research: a descriptor-based MLP (\texttt{base}), a Random Forest on the same descriptors (\texttt{RF}), two language-model baselines (\texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}), a graph-based baseline (\texttt{GraphDTA-style}), and a cross-attention baseline (\texttt{DrugBAN}). This panel is evaluated on seven benchmarks organized into three families: synthetic cold-start (\texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}), temporal/provenance (patent and non-patent BindingDB time splits), and supporting target-cold OOD (\texttt{BindingDB\_Ki}, \texttt{DAVIS}).

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Ranking non-transferability.} We show that model rankings on synthetic cold-start splits do not transfer to temporal/provenance benchmarks: the identity of the best model changes across evaluation axes, and this is confirmed with paired bootstrap confidence intervals.
    \item \textbf{Provenance separation.} By constructing both patent-derived and non-patent temporal benchmarks, we demonstrate that the ranking change is not merely a patent-provenance artifact—it persists on non-patent time-split data.
    \item \textbf{Overlap-sensitive diagnosis.} We introduce overlap-restricted temporal variants and overlap-bucket analyses that reveal the temporal shift is \emph{structured}: different train--test overlap strata favor different model families, explaining why aggregate rankings are unstable.
    \item \textbf{Reusable benchmark resources.} We release split manifests with stable \texttt{example\_id} keys and a standalone evaluator, enabling external models to be scored on the exact partitions used in this study.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Cold-start evaluation in DTI}

The evaluation of DTI models under cold-start conditions was formalized by \citet{pahikkala2015toward}, who defined four settings—S1 (warm), S2 (drug-cold), S3 (target-cold), and S4 (double-cold)—and showed that performance drops sharply from S1 to S4. This taxonomy is now standard: most recent DTI methods report at least one cold-start variant \citep{ahmed2024dtilm,luo2024druglamp,zhao2025coldstartcpi,liu2025spdti}. However, published evaluations typically optimize within a single benchmark design. \citet{wang2021unified} provided an early demonstration that the winner can change across settings—DeepDTI led in warm-start while Random Forest won in cold-drug—but this observation was incidental rather than the focus of a controlled benchmark study.

\subsection{Split design and data leakage in molecular machine learning}

The choice of train--test split has long been recognized as consequential in chemoinformatics. \citet{sheridan2013timesplit} showed that time-split cross-validation produces error estimates much closer to true prospective performance than random splits. \citet{yang2019chemprop} evaluated 850+ experiments across 19 public and 16 proprietary datasets and found that scaffold splits better approximated temporal splits than random splits, while model rankings could shift between split types. More recently, \citet{graber2025resolvingbias} demonstrated that train--test data leakage in PDBbind had \emph{severely} inflated deep-learning binding-affinity metrics; on their CleanSplit, apparent model superiority vanished. \citet{datasail2024} proposed optimization-based splitting to minimize information leakage, and \citet{scaffoldsplitoverestimate2024} showed that scaffold splits still permit high molecular similarity between train and test.

These findings establish that split design matters, but they focus on molecular property prediction or binding affinity regression rather than on binary DTI classification under cold-start versus temporal shifts.

\subsection{Temporal and prospective validation}

Temporal evaluation—training on past data and testing on future data—is regarded as the gold standard for estimating prospective performance. \citet{sheridan2013timesplit} introduced this idea for QSAR at Merck, and \citet{simpd2023} later proposed an algorithm to simulate time splits on public datasets based on Novartis lead-optimization projects. The Therapeutics Data Commons provides a DTI domain-generalization benchmark with patent-based temporal splits \citep{huang2022tdc}, where out-of-distribution performance degrades substantially. However, no prior study has directly compared the model rankings produced by synthetic cold-start splits with those from temporal/provenance-shifted evaluation on the same model panel and dataset family.

\subsection{Benchmark sensitivity across molecular ML}

Several large-scale studies have demonstrated benchmark sensitivity beyond DTI. \citet{wu2018moleculenet} established MoleculeNet but its rankings are now considered unreliable due to dataset quality issues. \citet{luosystematicstudy2023} trained 62,820 models and found that ``discrepancies in the actual data split across the literature can entail unfair performance comparison.'' \citet{welqrate2024} showed substantial sensitivity to dataset quality, featurization, and splitting in small-molecule benchmarks. \citet{ektefaie2024generalizability} showed that in-distribution performance is a poor proxy for OOD performance across molecular tasks. \citet{benchmarkingcompoundactivity2024} found that SOTA models ``do not work well consistently'' on novel realistic endpoints and that classical descriptors sometimes outperform learned representations.

\subsection{Positioning of this work}

The general principle that benchmark design affects model ranking is well established. Our contribution is to \emph{instantiate and quantify} this principle for DTI prediction in a way that has not been done before: (a)~we directly compare synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance rankings on the same six-model panel; (b)~we separate temporal shift from provenance confounding using paired patent and non-patent benchmarks; and (c)~we introduce overlap-sensitive diagnosis that reveals the temporal shift is structured rather than monolithic. The emphasis is on validation methodology rather than on proposing a new architecture.

\section{Benchmark Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Model panel}

We deliberately selected six models spanning the major architectural families in DTI to ensure that benchmark sensitivity is not confined to one model type:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{base}} — descriptor-based MLP. Drug: RDKit Morgan fingerprint (2048 bits). Target: hashed protein k-mer vector (2048 dims). Pair feature: $[d, t, d \odot t, |d-t|]$, fed to a 2-layer MLP head. This is the anchor system for all delta comparisons; its full specification is in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:base-spec}.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{RF}} — Random Forest on the \emph{same} Morgan+k-mer descriptors as \texttt{base}, isolating the effect of the learning algorithm from the representation.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{DTI-LM}} — pooled ChemBERTa + ESM-2 features with a downstream MLP \citep{ahmed2024dtilm}. Represents the language-model pooling family.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{HyperPCM}} — task-conditioned variant where the target branch generates conditioning parameters ($\gamma$, $\beta$, weight, bias) for the drug representation. Represents the hypernetwork/conditioning family.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GraphDTA-style}} — molecular GCN encoder + amino-acid CNN encoder \citep{nguyen2021graphdta}. Represents the graph-based family.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{DrugBAN}} — bilinear attention network that computes explicit drug-substructure $\times$ protein-subsequence interactions \citep{bai2023drugban}. Represents the cross-attention interaction family, which models fine-grained cross-modal pairing rather than simple concatenation.
\end{itemize}

This panel is not exhaustive—important recent architectures such as ColdstartCPI \citep{zhao2025coldstartcpi} and SP-DTI \citep{liu2025spdti} are not included—but it covers six distinct architectural families, which is sufficient to demonstrate that benchmark sensitivity is a cross-family phenomenon.

\textbf{Training fairness.} To ensure that ranking differences reflect benchmark semantics rather than tuning asymmetry, we applied a uniform optimization protocol across all neural models: AdamW with learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, BCE-with-logits loss with class-balanced positive weighting, and early stopping by validation \AUPRC{} within a fixed epoch budget (6--8 epochs). Hyperparameters were not individually tuned per model--benchmark combination; each architecture uses a single configuration across all benchmarks (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:base-spec} and \ref{tab:graph-spec}). For \texttt{DTI-LM} and \texttt{HyperPCM}, the pretrained language-model encoders (ChemBERTa and ESM-2) are frozen; only the downstream MLP is trained. \texttt{RF} uses \texttt{scikit-learn} defaults with $400$ estimators and balanced class weighting. This design choice sacrifices per-model peak performance in favor of commensurable tuning effort, which is the appropriate trade-off for a study whose conclusion is about ranking stability rather than absolute performance. The reported rankings should therefore be interpreted as reflecting a fixed-tuning-budget regime; individual models might achieve higher absolute performance under dedicated tuning, but the ranking instability across benchmark axes is unlikely to vanish since it arises from benchmark-level shift rather than hyperparameter sensitivity.

\subsection{Benchmark taxonomy}

We organize benchmarks into three families (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}):

\textbf{Synthetic cold-start} (\texttt{BindingDB\_Kd}). Three split definitions—\texttt{unseen\_drug} (S2 in the Pahikkala taxonomy), \texttt{unseen\_target} (S3), and \texttt{blind\_start} (S4, both drug and target unseen)—each removing different forms of entity overlap. These are the standard evaluation settings used in most recent DTI papers.

\textbf{Temporal/provenance}. Two year-based BindingDB benchmarks: a \emph{patent} split (\texttt{patent\_temporal}), where training uses patents filed before a cutoff year and testing uses later patents; and a \emph{non-patent} split (\texttt{nonpatent\_temporal}), constructed from the official BindingDB release after excluding patent-derived entries. The non-patent benchmark is critical because it controls for the patent-provenance confound: if rankings change only on the patent benchmark, the effect might be driven by patent curation rather than temporal shift per se. We describe both as ``temporal/provenance OOD'' because even the non-patent benchmark mixes time shift with changes in assay composition and journal sourcing.

\textbf{Supporting target-cold OOD}. \texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} provide shifted target-cold settings that test stability across assay and dataset boundaries.

Within the temporal family, we further construct two \emph{overlap-restricted variants}: \texttt{patent\_temporal\_pair\_novel} (test pairs whose exact drug--target combination is absent from training) and \texttt{patent\_temporal\_drug\_novel} (test pairs whose drug scaffold is absent from training). These variants allow us to test whether conclusions hold when entity or pair recurrence is explicitly suppressed.

\subsection{Metrics and uncertainty}

We report \AUPRC{} as the primary metric and \AUROC{} as a secondary check. \AUPRC{} is emphasized because the central question concerns imbalanced ranking quality. Because prevalence ranges from 0.06 (\texttt{DAVIS}) to 0.57 (patent), absolute \AUPRC{} is compared only \emph{within} a benchmark. All benchmarks use matched three-seed evaluation. Paired bootstrap confidence intervals (10,000 resamples) support the principal ranking comparisons; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap-summary} collects these intervals.

\subsection{Overlap diagnosis}

For diagnostic analysis, each test example carries a saved \texttt{overlap\_score} defined as $\min(s_d, s_t)$, where $s_d$ and $s_t$ are the cosine similarities between the test drug/target and their nearest training-set neighbors. We partition test examples into three equal-sized overlap buckets and report per-bucket performance. This diagnosis reveals \emph{which overlap regime} drives the aggregate ranking, and whether different model families dominate in different strata.

\subsection{Reusable resources}

Each split is exported as a canonical bundle. The release includes: (i)~split manifests with versioned metadata, split counts, and prevalence; (ii)~partition tables (\texttt{train.csv}, \texttt{test.csv}) with stable \texttt{example\_id} keys and exported overlap scores; (iii)~entity tables with unique drug and target vocabularies; (iv)~per-model prediction CSVs with per-sample probabilities; (v)~model configuration files and training seeds; and (vi)~a standalone evaluator (\texttt{scripts/evaluate\_prediction\_csv.py}) that scores external predictions against the exact test partitions used in this study. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:resource-release} lists the artifacts. A DOI-backed archive will be deposited at acceptance.

\subsection{Even synthetic cold-start rankings disagree}

We begin with the \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} synthetic panel (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). The key finding is that no single model dominates all three cold-start settings. On \texttt{unseen\_drug}, \texttt{RF} achieves the highest mean \AUPRC{} ($0.7339$), substantially ahead of all neural models—including the cross-attention \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.5856$), which ranks second among neural approaches but still trails \texttt{RF} by $>0.15$. On \texttt{blind\_start}, \texttt{RF} again leads ($0.4512$), with \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.4111$) competitive but highly variable across seeds. However, on \texttt{unseen\_target}, \texttt{base} is strongest ($0.5224$) and the \texttt{RF}--\texttt{base} difference is not significant (paired bootstrap CI crosses zero); here \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.4219$) drops below most other models.

This already challenges the common practice of reporting a single cold-start result: the winner on drug-cold is not the winner on target-cold, even within the same dataset. The effect is not confined to one model family—it spans all six architectures and reflects the different demands that drug-side versus target-side extrapolation place on the representation and the learner.

A scaffold-restricted control sharpens this further. When entity holdout is replaced with a Murcko-scaffold split (\texttt{scaffold\_drug}), the earlier \texttt{HyperPCM} edge over \texttt{base} on the drug side vanishes: the paired bootstrap CI for \texttt{HyperPCM}$\,-\,$\texttt{base} crosses zero (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness-probes}). The synthetic panel is useful, but its apparent best model depends on how chemically strict the split is.

\subsection{Temporal/provenance benchmarks change the ranking}

We next evaluate the same panel on the primary temporal/provenance benchmarks (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}, last two columns). On \texttt{patent\_temporal}, \texttt{RF} achieves the highest \AUPRC{} ($0.8234$), followed by \texttt{DTI-LM} ($0.7825$) and \texttt{base} ($0.7772$). The paired bootstrap intervals for \texttt{RF}$\,-\,$\texttt{base} and \texttt{RF}$\,-\,$\texttt{DTI-LM} are both strictly positive. The key observation is \emph{not} that \texttt{RF} wins—it is that the ranking differs from the synthetic panel (where \texttt{base} led on target-cold) and that the ordering among neural models also shifts (\texttt{DTI-LM} now outperforms \texttt{HyperPCM}, reversing the blind-start pattern).

The non-patent temporal benchmark strengthens this conclusion. On \texttt{nonpatent\_temporal}, \texttt{RF} again leads ($0.7201$), with \texttt{GraphDTA-style} ($0.6635$), \texttt{DTI-LM} ($0.6531$), and \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.6289$) above \texttt{base} ($0.6369$). Because this benchmark excludes patent-derived data, the ranking change cannot be attributed solely to patent-provenance curation. The temporal/provenance shift genuinely produces different model orderings from the synthetic cold-start panel.

\subsection{Overlap policy within the temporal family matters}

Even within the patent temporal family, the winner is sensitive to how much entity or pair recurrence is allowed (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness-probes}). In the \texttt{pair\_novel} variant—where all test drug--target pairs are absent from training—\texttt{DTI-LM} becomes the \AUPRC{} leader ($0.7491$), overtaking \texttt{RF} ($0.7393$), and the paired bootstrap CI for \texttt{DTI-LM}$\,-\,$\texttt{RF} is strictly positive. In the \texttt{drug\_novel} variant, \texttt{DTI-LM} again leads ($0.7402$ vs.\ $0.7356$ for \texttt{RF}).

This is an important finding: the identity of the best model changes not only across benchmark families but also across overlap policies \emph{within} a single temporal benchmark. It implies that aggregate temporal rankings can be dominated by the easy, high-overlap portion of the test set, and that the model best suited for genuinely novel predictions may differ from the aggregate winner.

An earlier-cutoff probe (\texttt{patent\_temporal\_v2017}) preserves the \texttt{DTI-LM}$\,>\,$\texttt{base} ordering (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:patent-v2017-results}), indicating that the effect is not an artifact of a particular year boundary.

\subsection{The temporal shift is structured, not monolithic}

To understand \emph{why} temporal rankings differ from synthetic ones, we diagnose the patent benchmark by year band and overlap bucket (Table~\ref{tab:patent-diagnosis-compact}, Figure~\ref{fig:patent-diagnosis}).

\textbf{Year bands.} \texttt{RF} leads in both the large 2019 slice and the pooled 2020--2021 slice. The temporal shift does not concentrate in one particular time period.

\textbf{Overlap buckets.} This is the most revealing analysis. In the \emph{lowest-overlap} bucket (test examples least similar to training), \texttt{DTI-LM} is the strongest model ($0.6353$), ahead of \texttt{RF} ($0.6005$). But in the \emph{middle-} and \emph{high-overlap} buckets, \texttt{RF} dominates ($0.7606$ and $0.9426$). Because the high-overlap stratum is large, \texttt{RF} wins the aggregate benchmark. However, a practitioner interested in truly novel predictions—the low-overlap regime—would select \texttt{DTI-LM} instead.

This structured pattern explains the instability: aggregate rankings are a mixture over heterogeneous difficulty strata, and different models specialize in different strata. The temporal shift is not simply ``harder cold-start.'' It changes the composition of easy and hard examples, which changes which model's strengths matter most.

\subsection{Supporting benchmarks confirm heterogeneity}

\texttt{BindingDB\_Ki / unseen\_target} and \texttt{DAVIS / unseen\_target} provide external checks on ranking stability (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:support}). On \texttt{Ki}, \texttt{base} leads ($0.6934$), ahead of all other models including \texttt{DTI-LM} ($0.6648$), \texttt{RF} ($0.6328$), and \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.6121$). On \texttt{DAVIS}, \texttt{RF} regains the lead ($0.4408$), while \texttt{DrugBAN} ($0.3164$) falls to the bottom. Neither benchmark recovers a stable winner, reinforcing the main finding. These supporting benchmarks should not be over-interpreted as direct evidence for the temporal/provenance argument, since they differ from the main \texttt{BindingDB\_Kd} setting in assay type and dataset source; their role is to demonstrate that ranking instability extends beyond the primary benchmark family.

\textbf{Metric robustness.} The ranking instability documented above is not an artifact of the \AUPRC{} metric. Across the five primary benchmarks, the identity of the best model under \AUROC{} agrees with \AUPRC{} in four of five cases (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). A Kendall's $W$ rank concordance analysis confirms low cross-benchmark agreement under both threshold-free metrics: $W = 0.378$ (\AUPRC{}) and $W = 0.374$ (\AUROC{}) across all five benchmarks (Appendix Section~\ref{sec:rank-concordance}). The ranking non-transferability conclusion is therefore supported by two independent threshold-free metrics. Screening-oriented metrics such as enrichment factor or BEDROC would add useful context for low-prevalence benchmarks like \texttt{DAVIS}, but these require threshold or weighting choices that introduce additional benchmarking decisions; we leave them to future extensions.

%% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Implications for DTI model selection}

The central message is straightforward: a single synthetic cold-start split is not sufficient evidence for DTI generalization. Our six-model panel shows that the winner changes across (a)~cold-start regimes, (b)~synthetic versus temporal/provenance evaluation, and (c)~overlap policies within a temporal benchmark. Three reporting recommendations follow:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item DTI studies should report at least one synthetic \emph{and} one temporal/provenance-shifted benchmark when making generalization claims.
    \item Overlap characteristics should be disclosed, because entity or pair recurrence can change the apparent ranking even within one benchmark family.
    \item Claims about the superiority of any architecture family (descriptor-based, LM, graph, cross-attention) should be phrased in benchmark-qualified terms.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Random Forest frequently wins}

An unexpected empirical pattern deserves interpretation. \texttt{RF} leads on synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}, \texttt{blind\_start}, and both temporal/provenance panels, yet trails on \texttt{unseen\_target} and \texttt{Ki}. Because \texttt{RF} and \texttt{base} use identical descriptors, the pattern is not about representation. We interpret it as \emph{descriptor-space interpolation under drug-side support}: when held-out drugs remain similar to training scaffolds (median nearest-train Tanimoto 0.77 on \texttt{unseen\_drug}), tree ensembles exploit local descriptor neighborhoods effectively. When the dominant challenge shifts to protein-side extrapolation, the hashed k-mer representation becomes the bottleneck and the \texttt{RF} advantage vanishes.

This interpretation is consistent with the overlap-bucket diagnosis: \texttt{RF} dominates the high-overlap strata where interpolation is easiest, while \texttt{DTI-LM}'s learned representations become advantageous in the low-overlap regime. The recurrent \texttt{RF} gains should therefore not be read as ``classical beats deep learning'' but as evidence that descriptor-level interpolation suffices when drug-side support is high—a regime-specific finding rather than a universal one.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Panel scope.} Six models spanning six architectural families provide sufficient evidence for ranking instability, but the panel is not exhaustive. Recent architectures such as ColdstartCPI \citep{zhao2025coldstartcpi}, DrugLAMP \citep{luo2024druglamp}, and SP-DTI \citep{liu2025spdti} are not included. A larger community benchmark would be needed to quantify how far this effect extends.

\textbf{Confounded temporal shift.} The patent benchmark mixes temporal, provenance, assay, and possibly label-noise shifts. We do not claim to isolate which factor drives the ranking change; our claim is that the \emph{aggregate} temporal/provenance evaluation produces different rankings from synthetic cold-start, which is the practically relevant comparison for model selection. The non-patent benchmark partially controls for provenance and preserves the ranking change, but it is smaller (7,174 vs.\ 49,028 rows). A cleaner causal attribution would require source-matched temporal cohorts with harmonized assay composition.

\textbf{Seed count and metrics.} Three seeds are sufficient for this validation study but not generous; high-variance settings (e.g., synthetic \texttt{unseen\_drug}) would benefit from more seeds. For low-prevalence benchmarks like \texttt{DAVIS}, enrichment metrics (e.g., BEDROC) would add screening-specific context.

\textbf{No prospective validation.} We do not evaluate biological decision quality. The results demonstrate ranking instability under retrospective evaluation designs, not prospective predictive value.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented a controlled benchmark study showing that model rankings in DTI prediction are not preserved across evaluation axes. Across a six-model panel, synthetic cold-start splits already produced different winners depending on which entity is held out. Temporal/provenance benchmarks changed the ranking further, and this effect survived on non-patent data, ruling out a patent-provenance artifact. Within the temporal family, overlap-restricted variants and overlap-bucket diagnosis revealed that the shift is structured: different overlap strata favor different model families. These findings establish that synthetic cold-start and temporal/provenance benchmarks are non-interchangeable validation settings for DTI. Future studies should report both axes when making generalization claims, and benchmark-qualified language should replace architecture-level universal claims.

\label{sec:rank-concordance}

  \centering

  \caption{Rank concordance analysis. Kendall's $W$ ranges from 0 (no agreement) to 1 (perfect agreement). Spearman $\rho$ is shown for selected benchmark pairs. All analyses use the six-model panel.}
  \label{tab:rank-concordance}
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{3.5pt}
  \begin{tabular*}{\textwidth}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}lcc@{}}
    \toprule
    Statistic & \AUPRC{} & \AUROC{} \\
    \midrule
    Kendall's $W$ (all 5 benchmarks) & 0.378 ($p=0.09$) & 0.374 ($p=0.10$) \\
    Kendall's $W$ (synthetic only, 3 benchmarks) & 0.416 & 0.365 \\
    Kendall's $W$ (temporal only, 2 benchmarks) & 0.771 & 0.914 \\
    \midrule
    Spearman $\rho$: drug-cold vs.\ target-cold & $-0.26$ & $-0.43$ \\
    Spearman $\rho$: drug-cold vs.\ patent temporal & $-0.09$ & $-0.03$ \\
    Spearman $\rho$: target-cold vs.\ patent temporal & $+0.83$ & $+0.77$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular*}

Ranks are computed from the mean metric value across three seeds for each model--benchmark pair; ties are resolved by midrank. Kendall's $W$ is computed over the six models ranked across either all five or a subset of benchmarks. The low $W$ values ($\approx 0.37$--$0.38$) confirm that model rankings are not well preserved across benchmarks under either metric. Drug-cold and target-cold rankings are negatively correlated ($\rho = -0.26$ to $-0.43$), indicating that these two synthetic settings reward opposite model strengths. Drug-cold and patent temporal rankings are essentially independent ($\rho \approx 0$), while target-cold and patent temporal are positively correlated ($\rho = +0.77$ to $+0.83$), suggesting that temporal shift shares some structure with protein-side extrapolation. The within-temporal concordance is much higher ($W = 0.77$--$0.91$), indicating that the two temporal benchmarks agree with each other more than either agrees with drug-cold evaluation.
